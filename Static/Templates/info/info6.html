inSight

As with previous Mars landers and rovers, the InSight mission relies on Mars-orbiting spacecraft to relay data from the spacecraft to the antennae of the Deep Space Network. There are two ways to communicate with the Earth, one of them is through the RISE antenna, and the other one is through the UHF antenna.
The rotation and interior structure experiment (RISE) works like a mirror and studies mars reflexes. Its antenna sends back signals sent from Earth to the lander. On Earth, the signal helps track the location of the lander to within centimeters. RISE tracks the lander's location over the entire mission to detect tiny changes in Mars wobble as it orbits the Sun. These observations help determine whether the core is liquid, and which other elements, besides iron, may be present.
UHF antenna: the lander has a helical “UHF antenna” that talks to Earth via Mars orbiters. Mars Recognition Orbiters (MRO) and the Mars Odyssey Orbiter are inSight’s primary relay orbiters; they pass in the sky over inSight twice per martian day. NASA’s Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution (MAVEN) orbiter and the European Space Agency trace gas orbiter and Mars express, are available as backup to help InSight communicate with Earth in case MRO and Odyssey are not available.
